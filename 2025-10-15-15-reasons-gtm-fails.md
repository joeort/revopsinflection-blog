We’ve established that [GTM is hard](https://revopsinflection.substack.com/p/gtm-is-hard?r=5daal5). You’re never done with it either.

When GTM Partners looked under the covers of the reasons why it was broken, 15 areas came to the surface. Some are easier to solve for than others. Take a look below and then let’s dive into this.

1.  Heroics - if your quota attainment has a lot of under-performers and 1 ultra-high performer, you’ll be in for a world of hurt if that top performer leaves or has a bad quarter (even the best fall short sometimes). You have to find a way to build for the consistency of the majority of your sales team. Quota attainment is typically modeled between 70-80%. If your attainment excluding that 1 rep is 50% or less, this is an urgent need for you to solve.
    
2.  Misalignment - as long as I’ve been in the B2B space, starting at SiriusDecisions (now Forrester), we’ve been talking about alignment. Back then, they did a study (this is early 2000s so don’t use it as a current data point) and they found that aligned companies grew 19% faster and had 16% higher profitability. Why has alignment eluded us so much over the past 20 years? A topic for a future post.
    
3.  Inaccuracy - great forecasts are within +/- 5% of the ending number. There is a lot of nuance around measuring accuracy (what number do you take? how far into the quarter? do you split new vs expand vs renewal? To improve at your forecasting takes a lot of moving parts all working jointly for the betterment of your forecasting. This includes a clear sales process, a solid sales methodology, integrated data (including all communications and sentiment analysis) and rep intuition.
    
4.  Value - discounts are a silent ARR killer and also reduces the value in the buyer’s eyes. I was once given a price from a vendor and then I told them I was paying half that from their closest competitor. They immediately came back and said they would match it. Is your product suddenly 50% less valuable? How do you connect the value of the offering to business impact at the buyer’s company (don’t forget about personal value as well!)
    
5.  ROI - being stuck trying to prove value to deliver at renewal time likely means you’re struggling across 2 key KPIs: GRR and NRR. I have a whole framework around ROI to work through with customers to drive incremental changes to the way we communicate our ROI. Getting out of the muddy middle and into higher levels of the framework set yourself up in a much better place for proving your impact on the business.
    
6.  Prioritization - are you laser focused on a mission or are you Dug from the movie Up (squirrel!)? If you make everything a priority, nothing is getting the time and effort they need to truly be successful. I’ve seen this multiple times where the ELT (executive leadership team) will say we’re focusing on 3 things and that’s it! And then show 6 initiatives under each of those 3 things (for the math deficient, that’s 18 initiatives). Slow is smooth. Smooth is fast.

7.  Strategy - do you have clarity, alignment and trust in the plan from the executive level down through the organization to the members of the team executing against that strategy? This is the basis for the [GTM OS](http://www.revopsinflection.com/gtm-os). How you communicate and execute against the executive strategy will make or break performance.
    
8.  Churn - is sometimes forgotten as teams focus their energy on acquisition but as you get larger, even a decent renewal rate (90%+) still means you’re losing 10% of your ARR in a given year. And if you’re at $100M ARR, that’s $10M walking out the door. Your acquisition needs to do at least $10M just to break even. Fixing churn is a key focus as your install base grows.
    
9.  Competition - as AI increases the speed at which the competition can be created and catch up on features/functionality, our approach with our competition needs attention. Create competitive dashboards, design battlecards, continually update your messaging and find ways to take them out.
    
10.  Cycle - although many buyers complete a lot of research in advance, your ability to be included in the discussion is something somewhat in your control. It comes down to brand and demand.
     
11.  Reactive - getting ahead of issues (churn, pipeline creation, rep attrition, etc) will impact your ability hit future goals. This is a cultural item to be addressed.
     
12.  Upmarket - it seems like everybody wants to move upmarket at some point. The bigger ASPs and potential for higher LTVs are attractive. What you’ve done to land and support the smaller companies don’t translate as you move up. You need different sales skills, improved product performance and better messaging to succeed upmarket.
     
13.  AR - an area where attention has waned, especially in the AIO season, but despite that “crash” Gartner had in 2025, analyst firms still swing big influence in the market. G2 is another area companies spend time trying to drive social proof.
     
14.  POV - many companies look and sound the same. How you differentiate your point of view will get more attention for you and your brand. This helps both on the offensive (acquisition) and defensive (retention) sides.
     
15.  Platform - expanding beyond an initial product (battling through Problem Market Fit to Product Market Fit) takes thoughtful expansion through new product development, M&A and expansion. As companies grow, more and more growth comes from customers. It’s key to understand what products drive the most value for your segments and execute against that with the right GTM motions (PLG, Inbound, Community, etc).
     

As you look at your company, what areas above do you see today? What have you had in the past and how did you overcome that challenge?
